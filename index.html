Virtual Center for the Study of Biotic Interactions (ViCSBI)



Climate change alters the seasonal timing and duration of ecologically and economically vital life cycle events in all species, including the onset and duration of flowering in plants, and the emergence time and activity periods of both beneficial and destructive insects. Climate and land-use changes (as well as introduced species) also affect the spatial distribution and interactions among species. Where the timing or distributions of interacting species respond differently to climate change, beneficial relationships (e.g., plants and their pollinators) and antagonistic relationships (e.g., agricultural vs. invasive plants; plants vs. their herbivores; animals vs. their parasites) can be disrupted, altering the diversity or function of wild and agricultural ecosystems, and threatening their persistence and productivity. We know much about the effects of climate change on the life cycles of individual species, but little about how it will alter beneficial or antagonistic interactions among species. This knowledge gap impedes our ability to forecast the effects of climate change on the sustainability of wild and agricultural ecosystems. Novel databases and data-driven models are needed to predict the effects of upcoming climate change on both the timing and the geographic distribution of interacting plant and insect species, and to integrate these effects to determine how biotic interactions will change over time and space.


Our research and training activities will leverage geographic and temporal data associated with millions of specimens housed in UC herbaria and museums, including genomic data that allow the building of phylogenies to understand the history of biotic interactions; we will then integrate these data with high- resolution climate data available for California. We will assemble the first integrated database that will enable the construction and testing of the statistical models needed to detect the effects of historical, contemporary, and future climate conditions on the seasonal timing and spatial distribution of hundreds of interacting plant- pollinator, plant-pest, and agricultural vs. invasive plant species pairs. We will use these models to forecast – and potentially to mitigate – the effects of environmental change on these interactions, which determine the economic stability of agricultural ecosystems in California and throughout the U.S.
